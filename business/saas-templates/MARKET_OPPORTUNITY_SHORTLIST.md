# Data-Centric SaaS Opportunity Shortlist

## Genesis

Created from user request: "I want to build something I care about, obtains
data and has been unicorn revenue generation wise. What should I be working
on?"

## Working Assumption About Founder Fit

Inference from active workspace areas:
- `journal/`
- `health/`
- `memory/`
- `data-ownership/`
- `communications/`
- `social-media/`

This suggests the strongest recurring interests are:
- personal data and quantified self workflows
- health and behavior change
- knowledge capture, memory, and follow-through
- creator / communication systems

That matters because the right idea is not just "big market"; it is a market
where you will still care after the novelty wears off.

## What Unicorn-Scale Data Businesses Usually Have in Common

The strongest revenue businesses in this class are not "just AI" or "just
analytics." They usually combine:

1. A hard-to-recreate data asset.
2. A workflow where decisions happen repeatedly.
3. A closed loop from data -> insight -> action -> better outcome.
4. Clear economic ROI, not vague inspiration.

Examples with current revenue evidence:

- `Datadog`: trailing twelve months ending March 31, 2025 revenue of
  `$2.8B`, built on operational telemetry and unified observability/security.
- `Klaviyo`: fiscal year 2025 revenue of `$1.234B`, built on first-party
  customer and commerce data.
- `Tempus`: full year 2025 revenue of `$1.27B`, with `Data and Applications`
  revenue of `$316.4M`, built on multimodal healthcare data.
- `Hims & Hers`: full year 2025 revenue of `$2.35B`, showing massive demand
  when health data and recurring care get paired with a strong consumer
  workflow.
- `Doximity`: fiscal year 2025 revenue of `$570.4M`, built on clinician
  network and workflow data.
- `Glean`: exceeded `$100M ARR` in 2025, built on enterprise knowledge and
  permissions-aware context.
- `Ashby`: grew revenue `135% YoY` and scaled from `1,300` to `2,700`
  customers by July 22, 2025, built on recruiting operations data and
  workflow depth.

## Ranked Shortlist

### Best Current Bet

`Health Operations OS for high-agency consumers`

Target user:
- people actively trying to improve sleep, energy, metabolic health, weight,
  recovery, ADHD management, or medication adherence

Data sources:
- wearable data
- sleep data
- CPAP data
- labs
- prescriptions
- meal / symptom logs
- calendar context
- journal notes

Narrow promise:
- turn scattered health exhaust into a weekly decision system:
  - what changed
  - what is likely driving it
  - what to try next
  - what to bring to a doctor

Why this is the best fit:
- highest founder resonance based on your existing behavior and repo surface
- genuinely data-native
- can start as a narrow, obsessive product instead of a hospital software
  company
- long-term market size is real, as shown by businesses like Hims, Doximity,
  and Tempus

Why now:
- consumer health data is more available than before
- model capability is finally strong enough to synthesize longitudinal data
- people increasingly expect personalized guidance, not just dashboards

Main risks:
- trust and safety are real constraints
- broad healthcare is a trap
- must start with a very narrow ICP and explicit non-diagnostic positioning

Recommended starting wedge:
- "weekly health intelligence for people with sleep / energy / medication
  issues who already collect data but cannot turn it into decisions"

### Possible

`Recruiting Ops Intelligence for lean talent teams`

Target user:
- recruiting leads and recruiting ops at companies that run many interviews
  but still manage too much by spreadsheet, ATS, and calendar glue

Data sources:
- ATS
- calendar
- email
- interviewer feedback
- pipeline metrics
- scorecards

Why it is attractive:
- clear willingness to pay
- strong recent company evidence (`Ashby`)
- high-frequency workflow with measurable ROI
- easier to monetize than health

Why it is not the top pick:
- weaker founder resonance than health / personal data
- more obvious competitive set

Good wedge:
- "decision support for recruiting ops: where the pipeline is leaking, where
  interview load is mismatched, and what action to take this week"

### Possible

`Founder / Operator Memory and Execution Graph`

Target user:
- founders and small teams drowning in email, meetings, notes, docs, and
  follow-ups

Data sources:
- email
- calendar
- docs
- notes
- CRM
- meeting transcripts

Why it is attractive:
- strong personal resonance
- very data-native
- enterprise appetite is real (`Glean` exceeded `$100M ARR` in 2025)

Why it is not the top pick:
- this category is crowded and increasingly abstract
- easy to drift into "AI second brain" vagueness

Good wedge:
- "follow-through OS for founder-led teams" rather than generic knowledge chat

## Do Not Pursue

- generic AI copilot for everyone
- generic second-brain / note-chat product
- pure creator analytics dashboards without a strong workflow
- broad healthcare platform on day 1

These all fail the same test: too broad, too legible, too easy to copy, or too
weakly tied to one painful recurring moment.

## Recommendation

If you optimize for what you actually care about and still want unicorn-scale
upside, work on:

`Health Operations OS for high-agency consumers`

If you optimize for easiest near-term B2B monetization instead of personal
resonance, work on:

`Recruiting Ops Intelligence`

That is the practical split.

## Why This Recommendation Is Defensible

- Health has huge proven revenue pools, not just venture hype.
- Your founder fit is stronger in health / personal data than in generic HR or
  sales tooling.
- The best data businesses start narrow, with one repeated decision loop.
- A narrow health-ops wedge can later expand into coaching, care navigation,
  adherence, clinician summaries, or employer / provider distribution.

## First Validation Step

Do not build a platform first.

Run a concierge validation loop:

1. Recruit `10` people who already track some combination of sleep, wearable,
   lab, or medication data.
2. Manually produce a weekly "what changed / what matters / what to try next"
   report for them.
3. Observe which inputs are actually useful and which are noise.
4. Charge for the report before automating heavily.

If people will not pay for the manual version, the product thesis is not ready.
